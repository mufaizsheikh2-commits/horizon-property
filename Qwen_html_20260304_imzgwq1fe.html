The Complete Home Buyer's Guide to Florida Real Estate

Purchasing a property in Florida represents one of the most significant financial decisions you'll make in your lifetime. Whether you're a first-time homebuyer, a seasoned investor, or someone relocating to the Sunshine State, understanding the complete buying process is essential to making informed decisions and securing the best possible outcome. This comprehensive guide will walk you through every step of the journey, from initial preparation to closing day and beyond.

Step 1: Financial Preparation and Mortgage Pre-Approval

Before you begin searching for properties, it's crucial to understand your financial position and obtain mortgage pre-approval. This initial step serves multiple purposes: it clarifies your realistic price range, demonstrates to sellers that you're a serious and qualified buyer, and gives you a competitive edge in Florida's often fast-paced luxury market where properties can receive multiple offers within days of listing.

Start by reviewing your credit report from all three major credit bureaus—Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. In Florida, most conventional lenders require a minimum credit score of 620, though scores of 740 or higher will secure you the most favorable interest rates. If you discover errors on your credit report, dispute them immediately, as the correction process can take 30-60 days. Pay down existing debts to improve your debt-to-income ratio, which lenders typically want to see below 43% for conventional loans.

Gather necessary financial documentation including two years of tax returns, recent pay stubs, bank statements, investment account statements, and documentation of any additional income sources. Self-employed buyers should be prepared to provide two years of profit and loss statements and business tax returns. Horizon Prime works with a network of trusted mortgage lenders who specialize in Florida real estate and can guide you through loan options including conventional mortgages, FHA loans, VA loans for veterans, jumbo loans for luxury properties, and portfolio loans for unique situations.

Understanding different loan types is essential. Conventional loans typically require 20% down payment to avoid private mortgage insurance (PMI), though some programs allow as little as 3% down. FHA loans, insured by the Federal Housing Administration, permit down payments as low as 3.5% but require mortgage insurance premiums. VA loans, available to eligible veterans and active military personnel, offer the significant advantage of zero down payment and no PMI requirement. Jumbo loans, necessary for properties exceeding conforming loan limits ($766,550 in most Florida counties as of 2024), typically require stronger credit profiles and larger down payments of 20-30%.

Step 2: Defining Your Property Criteria and Beginning Your Search

With pre-approval in hand, work with your Horizon Prime agent to define your property criteria in detail. Consider not just your immediate needs but your lifestyle goals for the next 5-10 years. How many bedrooms and bathrooms do you need now, and might you need in the future? Do you require a home office, guest suite, or space for aging parents? What's your commute tolerance, and which school districts matter to you? Do you prioritize waterfront access, golf course views, urban walkability, or suburban tranquility?

Your agent will set up a customized property search that automatically notifies you of new listings matching your criteria, often before they appear on public websites. In Florida's competitive market, speed is essential—the best properties often receive offers within 48-72 hours of listing. We'll also schedule private showings for properties that particularly interest you, allowing you to evaluate homes thoroughly without the pressure of open house crowds.

During property viewings, look beyond staging and décor to assess the home's fundamental qualities. Examine the roof age and condition, HVAC system functionality, plumbing and electrical systems, foundation integrity, and window quality. Consider the floor plan's functionality, natural light exposure, storage space, and potential for future renovations. Evaluate the neighborhood at different times of day, checking noise levels, traffic patterns, and overall ambiance. Your agent will provide comparative market analysis for each property, helping you understand whether the asking price aligns with recent sales of similar homes in the area.

Step 3: Making an Offer and Negotiation

When you find the perfect property, your Horizon Prime agent will help you craft a competitive yet strategic offer. This involves analyzing recent comparable sales (comps), current market conditions, days on market, and the seller's circumstances. In a seller's market with low inventory, you may need to offer at or above asking price, while in a buyer's market with ample inventory, there may be room for negotiation.

Your offer will include not just the purchase price but numerous other terms: your earnest money deposit (typically 1-3% of purchase price in Florida), proposed closing date, financing contingency period, inspection contingency period, appraisal contingency, and any personal property you want included in the sale. We'll also advise on escalation clauses, which automatically increase your offer up to a maximum if competing offers are received, and appraisal gap coverage, where you agree to cover the difference if the property appraises below your offer price.

Negotiation is both art and science. Your agent will present your offer professionally, highlighting your strengths as a buyer—strong pre-approval, flexible closing timeline, minimal contingencies. We'll negotiate not just on price but on terms that matter to you, whether that's a rent-back agreement allowing sellers to remain in the home after closing, inclusion of furniture or appliances, or repairs to address inspection findings. Our 15+ years of experience and knowledge of local market dynamics give us significant advantage in securing favorable terms for our clients.

Step 4: Due Diligence and Inspections

Once your offer is accepted, you enter the due diligence period—typically 10-15 days in Florida contracts. This critical phase allows you to thoroughly investigate the property and back out of the contract if significant issues are discovered, with full return of your earnest money deposit. Never skip or rush this step, even in competitive markets where sellers may pressure for shorter inspection periods.

Hire a licensed, experienced home inspector to conduct a comprehensive examination of the property's structure, systems, and components. The inspection should cover the roof, foundation, electrical systems, plumbing, HVAC, insulation, ventilation, windows, doors, and major appliances. In Florida, additional specialized inspections are often warranted: a four-point inspection (required by insurers for homes over 20-30 years old) examining roof, electrical, plumbing, and HVAC; wind mitigation inspection to identify features that reduce windstorm damage and qualify for insurance discounts; termite and wood-destroying organism inspection; mold inspection, particularly important in Florida's humid climate; and for waterfront properties, seawall and dock inspections.

Review the inspection report carefully with your agent. Not every issue requires repair or negotiation—normal wear and tear is expected in any home. However, significant defects like roof leaks, foundation problems, outdated electrical systems, or mold infestations warrant attention. You can request repairs, ask for a credit at closing to address issues yourself, renegotiate the purchase price, or in cases of severe problems, terminate the contract. Your agent will help you prioritize issues and negotiate strategically, understanding that sellers aren't obligated to fix everything but may be willing to address major concerns to keep the deal together.

During due diligence, also review all HOA documents if the property is part of a homeowners association or condominium. Examine the HOA's financial health, reserve funds, pending special assessments, rules and restrictions, insurance coverage, and meeting minutes. Ensure the HOA fees fit your budget and that you're comfortable with the community's regulations regarding rentals, pets, renovations, and exterior modifications.

Step 5: Securing Financing and Appraisal

While inspections are underway, your lender will order an appraisal to ensure the property's value supports the loan amount. The appraiser will visit the property, assess its condition and features, and compare it to recent sales of similar properties. In Florida's appreciating market, appraisal gaps—where the property appraises for less than the contract price—have become increasingly common. If this occurs, you'll need to either renegotiate the price with the seller, cover the difference with additional cash, or potentially lose your earnest money if you can't resolve the issue and have an appraisal contingency.

Maintain financial stability during this period. Don't make large purchases, open new credit accounts, change jobs, or make significant deposits to your bank accounts without documenting the source. Lenders will verify your financial status again just before closing, and any negative changes could jeopardize your loan approval. Respond promptly to any lender requests for additional documentation to keep the process moving smoothly toward your closing date.

Step 6: Final Walkthrough and Closing

One to two days before closing, conduct a final walkthrough of the property with your agent. Verify that agreed-upon repairs have been completed satisfactorily, that the property is in the same condition as when you made your offer (barring normal wear and tear), and that all included items like appliances, window treatments, and fixtures remain. Test major systems to ensure they're functioning properly. If you discover issues, address them immediately with your agent before proceeding to closing.

Closing day in Florida typically occurs at a title company or attorney's office. You'll review and sign numerous documents including the Closing Disclosure (which details all closing costs and should match your Loan Estimate received three days prior), the promissory note outlining your loan terms, the mortgage or deed of trust creating the lien on the property, and various affidavits and disclosures. You'll provide your certified funds for down payment and closing costs, typically via wire transfer.

Florida closing costs typically range from 2-5% of the purchase price and include title insurance (both owner's and lender's policies), documentary stamp taxes on the deed and mortgage, recording fees, survey costs, home inspection fees, appraisal fees, attorney fees, and prepaid items like property taxes and homeowners insurance. Your Horizon Prime agent will provide a detailed closing cost estimate early in the process so there are no surprises.

Once all documents are signed and funds are transferred, the title company records the deed with the county, officially transferring ownership to you. You'll receive the keys to your new home, often with a celebratory moment as you cross the threshold for the first time as a homeowner. Your agent will provide you with a comprehensive homeowner's manual including warranty information, utility contact numbers, maintenance schedules, and recommendations for trusted service providers.

But our relationship doesn't end at closing. Horizon Prime agents remain available to answer questions, provide referrals for contractors and service providers, and assist with future real estate needs. We celebrate this milestone with you and look forward to serving your real estate needs for years to come.

Understanding Florida-Specific Considerations

Buying property in Florida involves unique considerations that differ from other states. Hurricane insurance is essential and can be expensive, particularly in coastal areas. Many properties require separate flood insurance in addition to homeowners insurance, especially those in FEMA-designated flood zones. Understanding these insurance requirements and costs is crucial to determining true affordability.

Florida has no state income tax, which is a significant financial advantage for residents. However, property taxes can be substantial, though homestead exemption benefits are available for primary residences, exempting the first $50,000 of assessed value and capping annual assessment increases at 3% for homesteaded properties. Save Our Homes benefits can provide significant long-term tax savings for those who establish Florida residency.

For international buyers, Florida offers attractive investment opportunities with no restrictions on foreign ownership. However, international buyers should understand FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) withholding requirements, which mandate that buyers withhold 15% of the sales price when purchasing from foreign sellers, and should consult with tax advisors familiar with cross-border transactions.

The Complete Home Seller's Guide to Maximizing Value

Selling your property is likely one of the most significant financial transactions you'll undertake, and doing it correctly can mean the difference between leaving money on the table and achieving top dollar for your home. This comprehensive guide will walk you through every aspect of the selling process, from initial preparation through closing, with insights drawn from our 15+ years of experience selling over 4,500 properties across Florida's diverse markets.

Step 1: Choosing the Right Real Estate Agent

The agent you choose to represent your property will have the single greatest impact on your selling experience and final sale price. Don't simply select the agent who suggests the highest listing price or charges the lowest commission. Instead, look for a proven track record in your specific neighborhood and price range, strong marketing capabilities, excellent communication skills, and negotiation expertise.

Horizon Prime agents average sale prices 98% of asking price compared to the market average of 96%, and our properties sell 30% faster than the competition. This performance differential translates to thousands of dollars in additional net proceeds for our clients. We achieve these results through strategic pricing, professional marketing, extensive buyer networks, and skilled negotiation.

When interviewing potential agents, ask about their marketing plan, recent sales in your area, average days on market, list-to-sale price ratio, and communication protocols. Request references from past clients and actually contact them. Review sample marketing materials to assess quality. The right agent should provide a comprehensive comparative market analysis (CMA) showing recent sales, active competition, and market trends specific to your property type and location.

Step 2: Strategic Pricing and Market Positioning

Pricing your property correctly from the start is absolutely critical to achieving the best possible outcome. Overpricing is the most common and costly mistake sellers make. Properties priced above market value typically sit on the market for extended periods, becoming stigmatized as "stale listings." Buyers and their agents begin to wonder what's wrong with the property, and eventually, sellers must reduce the price—often below what they could have achieved with proper initial pricing.

Your Horizon Prime agent will prepare a detailed comparative market analysis examining recent sales of comparable properties (comps), currently active listings that represent your competition, and expired listings that failed to sell (often due to overpricing). We'll analyze price per square foot, days on market, absorption rates, and seasonal trends. We consider your property's unique features, condition, upgrades, location advantages, and any detriments that might affect value.

Strategic pricing involves understanding buyer psychology and search parameters. Most buyers search within specific price ranges—$475,000-$500,000, for example. Pricing at $500,000 captures buyers searching up to $500,000, while pricing at $505,000 excludes those buyers entirely, potentially eliminating 20-30% of your potential buyer pool. We'll advise on pricing strategies like pricing slightly below a round number to attract more buyers and potentially generate multiple offers that drive the price up through competition.

Market conditions significantly impact pricing strategy. In a seller's market with low inventory and high demand, you might price at or slightly above market value. In a buyer's market with ample inventory, competitive pricing becomes even more critical. Your agent will monitor market activity weekly and recommend price adjustments if necessary, though properly priced properties in good condition typically sell within the first few weeks of listing.

Step 3: Preparing Your Property for Sale

First impressions are everything in real estate, and they begin before buyers even step inside your home. Curb appeal—the exterior appearance of your property—sets the tone for the entire showing. Simple improvements like fresh paint on the front door, new house numbers, updated exterior lighting, trimmed landscaping, mulched flower beds, and a clean driveway can significantly enhance perceived value. For properties with dated exteriors, consider more substantial investments like new siding, roof replacement, or window upgrades, which often provide excellent return on investment.

Inside your home, decluttering and depersonalizing are essential. Remove excess furniture to make rooms feel larger and more spacious. Pack away personal items like family photos, religious artifacts, political items, and collections that might distract buyers or make them feel like guests in someone else's home rather than envisioning themselves living there. Clear countertops, organize closets (buyers will look), and remove about one-third of your belongings to create a sense of space and storage potential.

Deep cleaning is non-negotiable. Hire professional cleaners to scrub every surface, clean carpets, wash windows inside and out, detail appliances, and eliminate odors. Pay special attention to kitchens and bathrooms, which buyers scrutinize most carefully. Address minor repairs like leaky faucets, cracked tiles, sticking doors, burnt-out lightbulbs, and torn window screens. These small issues, while inexpensive to fix, can make buyers wonder what larger problems might exist.

Professional staging can dramatically impact both sale price and time on market. Staged homes typically sell for 6-20% more than unstaged homes and spend 73% less time on market, according to the National Association of Realtors. Staging highlights your home's best features, helps buyers understand how to use spaces effectively, and creates emotional appeal through carefully selected furniture, artwork, and accessories. Your Horizon Prime agent can provide staging consultation or connect you with professional stagers who specialize in your price range and property type.

Consider pre-listing inspections to identify issues before buyers discover them. While this involves upfront cost, it allows you to address problems on your timeline and budget rather than facing surprise repair requests during negotiations. You can provide inspection reports to potential buyers, demonstrating transparency and potentially speeding up their decision-making process.

In today's digital age, over 95% of home buyers begin their search online, making professional photography absolutely essential. Your listing photos are your property's first impression, and poor-quality images can cause buyers to skip your listing entirely, regardless of price or features. Horizon Prime invests in professional real estate photographers who specialize in architectural photography, using techniques like HDR (High Dynamic Range) imaging, twilight photography for dramatic exterior shots, and wide-angle lenses that showcase spaces effectively without distortion.

Beyond standard photography, we create comprehensive marketing packages including cinematic video tours that take viewers on an emotional journey through your property, 3D Matterport virtual tours allowing buyers to explore your home from anywhere in the world, drone photography and videography for properties with significant land, waterfront access, or impressive rooflines, and professional floor plans that help buyers understand the layout and flow of your home.

Your property will be listed on the Multiple Listing Service (MLS), which syndicates to hundreds of real estate websites including Zillow, Realtor.com, Trulia, and Redfin. But we go far beyond MLS exposure. We feature your property on our high-traffic website, promote it through targeted social media advertising on Facebook and Instagram reaching demographics likely to be interested in your property, send email blasts to our database of qualified buyers and agents, create custom property websites with detailed information and multimedia content, and for luxury properties, advertise in prestigious publications like Luxury Portfolio International, Christie's International Real Estate, and The Wall Street Journal's Mansion Global.

Real estate commission in Florida is typically paid by the seller and is negotiable, though the standard range is 5-6% of the final sale price for residential properties. This total commission is usually split between the listing agent (the seller's representative) and the buyer's agent, with each receiving 2.5-3%. For example, on a $500,000 home sale with a 6% commission, the total commission would be $30,000, with $15,000 going to the listing brokerage and $15,000 to the buyer's brokerage. Commercial real estate commissions vary more widely and may be structured differently, sometimes as a percentage of the sale price, a fixed fee, or a combination. While Florida law does not strictly require buyers to retain a real estate attorney for residential transactions, it is highly recommended, particularly for cash purchases, complex transactions, luxury properties, or if you're unfamiliar with Florida real estate law. The typical timeline from accepted offer to closing in Florida ranges from 30 to 60 days for financed purchases, though cash transactions can close in as little as 10-14 days if all parties are motivated and prepared. The timeline breaks down into several key phases: Once your offer is accepted and the contract is executed, the clock starts on various contingency periods. The inspection period in Florida is typically 10-15 days, during which you'll conduct home inspections, review HOA documents, and investigate the property thoroughly. This is your opportunity to back out of the contract with full earnest money refund if significant issues are discovered. Simultaneously, if you're obtaining financing, your lender will order the appraisal, which usually occurs within 7-10 days of contract execution. The appraisal must be completed and reviewed, and if the property appraises below contract price, you'll need to negotiate a solution, which can add time to the process. Your lender will also be processing your loan application, verifying employment and assets, and preparing underwriting conditions, which typically takes 21-30 days for conventional loans. The title company will conduct a title search, identify any liens or encumbrances, and prepare title insurance commitments, usually requiring 10-14 days. Once all contingencies are satisfied or waived, you'll receive a Closing Disclosure from your lender at least three business days before closing, as required by federal law. The final walkthrough typically occurs 24-48 hours before closing to verify property condition. Closing day itself usually takes 1-2 hours at the title company where you'll sign documents and provide funds. Cash buyers can expedite this process significantly since there's no lender involvement, no appraisal requirement (though still recommended), and fewer contingencies, allowing closings in 2 weeks or less if desired. Earnest money deposits in Florida typically range from 1% to 3% of the purchase price for residential transactions, though the exact amount is negotiable and can vary based on market conditions, purchase price, and buyer qualifications. In competitive markets or for luxury properties, sellers may request higher earnest money deposits of 3-5% to demonstrate buyer seriousness and financial strength. For example, on a $500,000 home purchase, a typical earnest money deposit would be $5,000-$15,000, while a $2 million luxury property might require $60,000-$100,000. The earnest money deposit serves several important purposes: it demonstrates to the seller that you're a serious buyer committed to completing the transaction; it provides the seller compensation if you breach the contract without legal justification; and it's typically applied toward your down payment and closing costs at closing, so it's not an additional expense but rather part of your total cash requirement. Earnest money is held in an escrow account, usually by the title company, listing brokerage, or attorney, and is protected until closing or contract termination. You can get your earnest money back if you terminate the contract within contingency periods (inspection, financing, appraisal) or if the seller breaches the agreement. However, if you back out after all contingencies have been removed without legal cause, you risk forfeiting your earnest money deposit. Cash buyers often provide larger earnest money deposits to strengthen their offers, while buyers using FHA or VA loans with minimal down payments might provide smaller deposits. Florida property taxes are assessed annually by county property appraisers and vary significantly by location, with effective tax rates typically ranging from 0.8% to 1.2% of assessed value, though some areas like Miami-Dade can exceed 1.5% when including municipal and school district levies. For a $500,000 home, annual property taxes might range from $4,000 to $7,500 depending on the specific location and millage rates. Florida offers significant property tax benefits through the homestead exemption, available to owners who establish the property as their primary residence as of January 1st of the tax year. The homestead exemption provides several valuable benefits: First, it exempts the first $25,000 of assessed value from all property taxes, including school district taxes. Second, it provides an additional exemption of up to $25,000 for assessed values between $50,000 and $75,000, though this additional exemption doesn't apply to school district taxes. Most importantly, the Save Our Homes (SOH) assessment limitation caps annual increases in assessed value for homesteaded properties at the lesser of 3% or the Consumer Price Index, regardless of how much market values increase. This benefit compounds over time, creating substantial tax savings for long-term residents. For example, if you purchase a home for $500,000 and market values increase 10% annually while your assessed value increases only 3% annually due to SOH, after 10 years your market value might be $1.3 million while your assessed value for tax purposes is only around $670,000, resulting in significant tax savings. To claim homestead exemption, you must file an application with your county property appraiser between January 1st and March 1st of the year you're claiming the exemption. Additional exemptions are available for seniors over 65, disabled persons, veterans, and widows/widowers. Your Horizon Prime agent can provide specific tax information for properties you're considering and guide you through the homestead exemption application process after closing.

Understanding the differences between condominiums, cooperatives, and townhouses is essential for Florida buyers, as each ownership structure has distinct legal, financial, and lifestyle implications. Condominiums are the most common form of attached ownership in Florida. When you buy a condo, you own your individual unit outright (as indicated by a deed) and share ownership of common areas like lobbies, hallways, elevators, pools, and grounds with other unit owners through the homeowners association (HOA). You pay monthly HOA fees that cover maintenance of common areas, building insurance, reserves for major repairs, and often amenities like security, trash removal, and water/sewer. Condo associations are governed by a board of directors elected by unit owners and must comply with Florida's Condominium Act, which provides specific protections and requirements. Townhouses typically involve owning both the interior of your unit and the land it sits on, including any private yard or patio. You still pay HOA fees for maintenance of common areas and exterior building maintenance in many cases, but you have more ownership rights and responsibilities than condo owners. Townhouse HOAs are generally less restrictive than condo associations regarding rentals, pets, and modifications. Cooperatives (co-ops) are relatively rare in Florida compared to New York but do exist, particularly in some older buildings. In a co-op, you don't actually own real property; instead, you own shares in a corporation that owns the entire building, and your share allocation gives you the right to occupy a specific unit through a proprietary lease. Co-op boards typically have extensive approval rights over prospective buyers, can impose strict restrictions on rentals and subletting, and monthly maintenance fees often include property taxes and underlying mortgage payments on the building. Financing co-ops can be more difficult as not all lenders offer co-op loans. Each ownership type has advantages and disadvantages: condos offer low-maintenance living and amenities but come with HOA fees and restrictions; townhouses provide more space and ownership rights while still offering some shared maintenance; co-ops can be more affordable but come with significant board control and financing challenges. Your Horizon Prime agent will help you understand the specific HOA documents, financial health, reserves, and restrictions for any property you're considering to ensure it aligns with your lifestyle and investment goals.

Florida's unique geographic and climate conditions create specific insurance requirements and considerations that differ significantly from most other states. All Florida homeowners with a mortgage are required to maintain homeowners insurance (also called hazard insurance) that covers the dwelling structure against perils like fire, theft, vandalism, and windstorm. However, standard homeowners policies in Florida typically exclude or severely limit flood damage and may have separate windstorm deductibles. Flood insurance is required by lenders for properties located in FEMA-designated Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs), which include many coastal and low-lying areas throughout Florida. Even if not required, flood insurance is highly recommended for all Florida homeowners, as standard policies don't cover flood damage and approximately 25% of flood claims occur outside high-risk flood zones. Flood insurance is available through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or private insurers, with costs varying dramatically based on flood zone designation, elevation, building characteristics, and coverage amounts. Windstorm insurance is another critical consideration in Florida. Many insurers offer policies with separate windstorm deductibles, typically 2-10% of the dwelling coverage amount rather than the standard $500-$1,000 deductible for other perils. For example, on a $500,000 home with a 2% windstorm deductible, you'd pay the first $10,000 of wind damage before insurance coverage begins. Some coastal areas have limited insurance options and may require coverage through Citizens Property Insurance Corporation, Florida's insurer of last resort, which is generally more expensive than private carriers. Hurricane insurance isn't a separate policy but rather refers to coverage for hurricane damage, which may involve both your homeowners policy (for wind damage) and flood insurance (for storm surge and flooding). Additional coverages to consider include water backup coverage for sewer or drain backups, ordinance or law coverage for code upgrade requirements after damage, and increased replacement cost coverage to ensure adequate protection in case of total loss. Insurance costs in Florida have increased significantly in recent years due to hurricane losses, litigation, and reinsurance costs, with average premiums now exceeding $2,000-$4,000 annually and coastal properties often paying $6,000-$10,000 or more. Your Horizon Prime agent can provide insurance cost estimates during the buying process and connect you with independent insurance agents who specialize in Florida coverage to help you find appropriate protection at competitive rates.

Home inspections in Florida require special attention to issues particular to the state's climate, geography, and building practices. While a general home inspection covers fundamental systems and structures throughout the home, Florida buyers should ensure their inspector is experienced with Florida-specific concerns and consider additional specialized inspections. The general home inspection should thoroughly examine the roof for age, condition, remaining life, and any signs of leaks or storm damage. Florida's intense sun, heavy rains, and hurricane exposure make roof condition critical, and many insurers won't cover homes with roofs over 15-20 years old. The inspection should evaluate the HVAC system's age, condition, and efficiency, particularly important in Florida's hot, humid climate where air conditioning is essential. Electrical systems should be checked for outdated components like Federal Pacific or Zinsco panels known for safety issues, aluminum wiring, and adequate capacity for modern electrical demands. Plumbing inspection should identify pipe materials (polybutylene pipes common in 1970s-1990s Florida homes are prone to failure), water heater age and condition, and any signs of leaks or water damage. The foundation and structure should be examined for cracks, settling, and water intrusion, particularly important in Florida's sandy soil conditions. Beyond the general inspection, Florida buyers should strongly consider: a four-point inspection, required by most insurers for homes over 20-30 years old, examining roof, electrical, plumbing, and HVAC systems; a wind mitigation inspection identifying features like roof shape, roof-to-wall connections, opening protections, and secondary water resistance that can qualify you for significant insurance discounts of 10-60%; termite and wood-destroying organism inspection, essential in Florida's warm, humid climate where termites are active year-round; mold inspection, particularly for homes with prior water damage, poor ventilation, or musty odors; seawall and dock inspection for waterfront properties; and pool inspection if the home includes a swimming pool.
